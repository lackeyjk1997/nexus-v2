[Participants: Ryan Foster (AE, Nexus/Anthropic), Marcus Thompson (Sales Manager, Nexus/Anthropic), Rachel Kim (Deal Desk Lead, Nexus/Anthropic), Mark Feldman (SVP Engineering, Cedarline Systems), Wei Zhang (Procurement Lead, Cedarline Systems)]

RYAN FOSTER: Thanks everyone. Quick intros since the room grew — Marcus is my manager, Rachel runs our deal desk so she can actually move on structure today. Mark you know, and Wei runs procurement at Cedarline. Mark, the technical recommendation landed where we hoped — do you want to set up where the commercial conversation stands?

MARK FELDMAN: I'll be direct because you've earned directness. Engineering's recommendation is you, unambiguously. The risk-reduction model Maya built on our highest-stakes automation was — it was the thing that moved it from conviction to a number I can defend. So the technology question is closed.

RACHEL KIM: That's great to hear, Mark. So what's the open question?

MARK FELDMAN: The open question is that my CFO has, as of two weeks ago, put a hold on all new vendor commitments over one million dollars pending a spend review. It's not aimed at us — it's a company-wide thing, we had a soft quarter and finance is tightening. But it's a real freeze, and at two-point-one we are squarely inside it. So even with engineering's recommendation, I cannot get a signature this quarter unless the spend review clears it or we restructure. That's the blocker, and I want to be honest it's a hard one.

WEI ZHANG: And procurement has to run the formal three-vendor comparison regardless, for anything this size. That's policy. So even setting the freeze aside, I have a process I'm required to run, and — I'll just say it — the Gemini option looks very cheap on my comparison sheet because of our existing Google commit. I'm not advocating, I'm telling you what the sheet says, because you should know what you're up against in the review.

MARCUS THOMPSON: Wei, I appreciate that a lot — knowing what the sheet says is half the battle. Can I ask: is the freeze a hard "no spend" or a "defer and justify" — because those are different problems and we'd solve them differently.

WEI ZHANG: It's defer-and-justify. The CFO's office will approve over-a-million commitments if there's a strong enough business case and the cash timing works. It's not "never," it's "convince us and maybe phase it."

RACHEL KIM: That's really useful, because phasing is something I can work with. Let me float a structure and you tell me if it helps with both the freeze and the comparison sheet. What if we structure it as a smaller committed first phase this quarter — scoped to the highest-stakes automation Mark mentioned, the one with the clearest risk-reduction number — with a ramp to the full commit next fiscal quarter once the spend review lifts. That gets you started inside what finance can approve now, ties the dollars to the automation with the hardest ROI, and the ramp is contractually pre-agreed so you're not re-negotiating.

MARK FELDMAN: That — yes. A phased structure tied to the highest-ROI automation is exactly the shape my CFO says yes to. It makes the first dollar the easiest dollar to defend.

WEI ZHANG: And on my comparison sheet, a phased commit changes the math too — the Gemini "free at the margin" story is strongest at the big all-at-once number. A phased, ROI-tied commitment lets me put your risk-reduction value next to their credits and have it be an actual comparison instead of a sticker-price loss.

MARCUS THOMPSON: I want to make sure we're not winning the structure and losing the substance, though. Wei, when the comparison sheet gets to the CFO, what's the one thing that, if it's missing, sinks us regardless of structure?

WEI ZHANG: Honestly? A defensible answer to "why not the cheaper bundled option." If the only answer is engineering's preference, finance overrides it half the time. If the answer is a quantified risk or cost the cheaper option carries, that holds.

MARK FELDMAN: Which is the legibility-and-reliability risk number. The cost of an opaque automated action firing wrong in production. That's the thing the Gemini credits don't price in.

RYAN FOSTER: Then that's the document that has to be airtight. Rachel, can we package the phased structure with the risk-reduction case as one finance-ready proposal, so Wei isn't assembling it across five emails?

RACHEL KIM: Yes. I'll draft a phased commercial proposal — phase one this quarter scoped to the priority automation, pre-agreed ramp next quarter — with the risk-reduction model attached as the "why not cheaper" answer, in one document Wei can drop straight into the spend review.

WEI ZHANG: If I get it as one finance-ready packet, I can get it on the CFO's review agenda for next cycle. Piecemeal, it misses the cycle and we're talking next quarter.

MARCUS THOMPSON: We'll get it to you as one packet this week. Mark, last thing from me — is there anyone in the CFO's orbit we should make sure sees the risk number directly, rather than hearing it secondhand through the comparison sheet?

MARK FELDMAN: The VP of Finance who owns the spend review. If she gets the risk-reduction logic firsthand, that's worth more than anything on Wei's sheet. Let me see if I can get her ten minutes when the packet lands.
